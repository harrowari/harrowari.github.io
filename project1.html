Modelling global terrestrial dust flux using cloud-based geospatial analysis.


Iron is an essential micronutrient for phytoplankton. Oceans account for the absorption of around a quarter of global CO2 emissions (Le Quéré et al., 2013). The nitrogen fixing activities of diazotrophs, sequestration of carbon and general ocean productivity are all limited by iron availability (Jickell et al., 2005; Falkowski et al., 2000; Maher et al., 2010). Around 500Mt of iron enters the oceans from the atmosphere every year (Shao et al., 2011). The impact of iron deposition is not uniform across the world’s oceans, depending on a range of factors, some of which are related to the chemistry and hence, origin of the material. Not all iron containing material can be utilised by marine biota. A large geographic range of iron solubilities exists, averaging 11% (Miao-Fan et al., 2006).


The trends in iron solubility and bioavailability based on its origin are not well understood, leading to uncertainty in global modelling. By incorporating data sets pertaining to iron content based on soil mineralogy, one can improve the outcome of dust models (Journet et al., 2014). Comparison of chlorophyll density with the origins of iron deposition on a global scale will allow for further evaluation of the nature of HNLC and LNLC regions (Martin, 1990; Calvo et al., 2004). 90% of Southern Ocean iron deposition is believed to originate from open scrublands (Ito, 2017). Further understanding of the origins of iron deposited to the world’s oceans will inform policy makers for future land use planning and sustainable land use management. Great uncertainty exists in predicting how anthropogenic activities will influence global dust emissions. Before imparting its effects on ocean biogeochemistry, terrestrial dust plays a key role in the formation of cloud condensation nuclei (CCN). Cloud processes can increase iron reactivity in dust as a result of the range pHs within this environment. Cloud processes are responsible for the mobilisation of iron, but to understand this, more research must be carried out into the quantification of the bioavailable iron fractions on land (Mackie et al., 2005).


An ever developing world presents large scale changes in land cover and use. Such changes result in increased intensity and frequency of dust storm events, an acute source of dust emission (IPCC, 2019). Furthermore, land use and cover changes promote wind erosion as a result of the removal of vegetation cover which serves to shelter exposed surfaces (Bestelmeyer et al., 2015). Anthropogenic activity is estimated to contribute between 10 and 60% of global dust loads and could increase (Mahowald et al., 2010; Shao et al., 2011; Webb et al., 2018). The rate and quantity of iron deposition to the oceans are important variables in controlling oceanic iron-limited processes. The bioavailability of material is a variable which has received less attention, given its complexity (Morel et al., 2008). The implementation of enrichment ratio approximation using size fractions, along with the albedo-based approach developed by Chappell and Webb, will offer significant improvements on past iron dust modelling studies. Dust emission is a key variable in the Earth’s ocean and atmosphere system and further understanding of the iron cycle is required to prepare for the effects of anthropogenic activities.
